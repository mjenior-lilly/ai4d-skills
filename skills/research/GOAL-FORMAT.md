# RESEARCH-GOAL.md Format

`RESEARCH-GOAL.md` is the compass for the research workflow. It defines the user's target understanding before any scout searches for sources.

## Template

```md
# Research Goal: {Topic or question}

## Core question
{The exact question the research must answer.}

## Intended use
{How the user will use the answer: decision, learning, strategy, design, writing, teaching, purchase, policy, etc.}

## Desired level of understanding
{orientation | working knowledge | decision-grade | expert map}

{One or two sentences explaining what that level means for this request.}

## Audience
{Who the synthesis is for and what they already know.}

## Scope
- {Facet or sub-question that must be covered}
- {Another required facet}

## Out of scope
- {Adjacent topic that should not be researched unless the goal changes}

## Source quality bar
- {Required source types, recency, authority, primary-source needs, geographic limits, language limits, or credibility constraints}

## Stopping criteria
- {Observable condition that means research is sufficient}
- {Minimum verified source count or facet coverage, if relevant}
- {Contradictions handled or remaining uncertainty made explicit}

## Final deliverable
{Expected output shape: research brief, comparison table, recommendation, literature map, annotated bibliography, etc.}
```

## Rules

- Define the goal before searching. A vague goal creates broad but shallow research.
- Tie source quality to intended use. Casual orientation can use high-quality secondary sources; decision-grade research usually needs primary or authoritative sources.
- Keep scope narrow enough for evidence to change the answer.
- Use exclusions aggressively. They protect the workflow from interesting but irrelevant tangents.
- Revise the goal only when the user changes direction or evidence shows the original framing is unusable. Record non-obvious revisions in `NOTES.md`.
